### 5.1. Legalization {status: partial, depth: 1, last_updated: 2026-05-25, dod: partial}

> **DoD status**: Partial. This pass captured the TP application route, the official digital-nomad temporary-stay route, citizenship anchor, and a first playbook. #### Now (until 03.2027)

**Temporary protection for Ukrainians.** The EU-level temporary-protection framework is extended to 04 March 2027 by Council Implementing Decision (EU) 2025/1460 [src-002]. Croatia's official Croatia4Ukraine / MUP application form captures the eligible categories for temporary protection, including Ukrainian citizens who resided in Ukraine on 24 February 2022, their family members, some stateless / third-country protection beneficiaries, Ukrainian citizens who fled shortly before 24 February 2022 because of the security situation, and unaccompanied minors [src-040]. The form is an application channel rather than a full policy explainer, so use it as evidence that Croatia maintains a dedicated TP intake path, while relying on the EU source for the 04 March 2027 horizon.

**Croatia digital-nomad temporary stay.** Croatia defines a digital nomad as a third-country national working through communication technology for a foreign employer or own company not registered in Croatia, and not working or providing services to Croatian employers [src-041]. Temporary stay can be granted for up to **18 months**; if granted for less than 18 months, an extension can be filed no later than 60 days before expiry and can bring the total to a maximum of 18 months [src-041]. A new digital-nomad stay application can be submitted 6 months after expiry of the previous digital-nomad / related stay [src-041].

The route is likely **not affordable at current income**. MUP says the applicant must have at least 2.5 average monthly net salaries from the previous year, increased by 10% of the average monthly net salary for each additional family member / life partner / informal life partner [src-041]. The current base amount stated by MUP is **€3,622.50/month**, or alternatively **€43,470** available for a 12-month stay and **€65,205** for an 18-month stay [src-041]. That base monthly floor is already above the couple's current ~$3,000/month income before adding the partner uplift.

**Application mechanics and documents.** MUP allows online application; it is forwarded to the police administration / station competent for the intended Croatian address [src-041]. Applicants can also apply at a Croatian embassy/consulate if outside Croatia, or at the competent police administration/station if legally in Croatia; in the latter case the applicant may remain in Croatia until the decision becomes enforceable [src-041]. Core documents captured in this pass: Form 1a for in-person filing, passport valid at least 3 months beyond intended stay, health insurance covering Croatia, proof of remote-work purpose, proof of means, criminal-record certificate from the home country or country of residence for more than one year before arrival, and address / intended address in Croatia [src-041]. Proof of health insurance, means, purpose, and criminal-record status must be in Croatian or English; translations must be by an authorised translator where needed, and the criminal-record proof must be legalized [src-041].

**Family / partner.** Close family members of a digital nomad granted temporary stay may join via family reunification [src-041]. MUP explicitly includes common-law partners, defined in the page as unmarried couples together for more than 3 years if childless, shorter if they have a child or in case of marriage [src-041]. This is more flexible than Cyprus / Italy, but the couple must be able to prove the relationship. If not, marriage may materially simplify the dependent route.

**Polish karta pobytu interaction.** Safe planning baseline: a Polish residence title does not substitute for Croatian residence authorization. If the Polish status is temporary protection, the EU one-Member-State TP principle remains relevant under the EU TP framework [src-002]. A Croatia-specific official explanation of Polish residence interaction was not captured in this pass.

#### After 4 March 2027

No Croatia-specific TP-to-ordinary-residence bridge equivalent to Greece's Article 194 was captured in this pass. The practical plan should therefore not rely on TP alone after the EU 04 March 2027 horizon [src-002]. Digital-nomad temporary stay is a fallback only if income rises above Croatia's threshold and the partner route is documented; however, it is a temporary stay with an 18-month maximum and a 6-month cooling-off period before a new application [src-041]. Ordinary work, business, self-employment, long-term-residence, and family routes need separate research.

#### Residence without local employer

The digital-nomad route explicitly supports remote work for a foreign employer or the applicant's own foreign / non-Croatian company and excludes work or services for Croatian employers [src-041]. This fits a foreign-client IT freelancer structurally, but the income threshold is currently the blocker. Local sole-proprietor / company-owner routes remain pending.

#### PR and citizenship

This pass did not extract Croatia's long-term-residence / permanent-stay requirements from an official source, so PR remains [verification required]. For citizenship, gov.hr states that foreign nationals may apply for Croatian citizenship by naturalisation and lists the broad legal basis, but detailed residence-year, language, and release-from-foreign-citizenship conditions still require a focused extraction in a later legalization iteration [src-042]. Treat citizenship timing as unresolved for now rather than assuming a specific number of years.

#### Personal playbook for our couple

1. **Use TP only as a temporary bridge, not the long-term plan.** EU TP is extended to 04 March 2027 [src-002], and Croatia has an official TP application path [src-040], but no post-TP conversion rule was captured.
2. **Do not plan on Croatia DN at the current budget.** The base DN means threshold is €3,622.50/month before partner uplift [src-041], above current ~$3,000/month income.

4. **Partner strategy: document cohabitation or marry.** Croatia's DN family route can include common-law partners, but the page requires a relationship exceeding 3 years if childless; marriage may be simpler if evidence is weak [src-041].
5. **Keep the Polish residence track separate.** It does not replace Croatian authorization; if it is TP, avoid assuming dual TP coverage under the EU one-Member-State rule [src-002].

### 5.3. Taxes {status: partial, depth: 1, last_updated: 2026-06-11, dod: partial}

> **DoD status**: Partial. This pass captures tax residence, ordinary self-employment PIT, personal allowances, social-security rates, VAT, filing mechanics, and a conservative USD 3,000/month worked example. Missing before passing DoD: exact 2026 self-employed contribution base for an IT freelancer / craft, whether the couple can safely use lump-sum craft taxation for foreign-client IT receipts, and accountant confirmation of VAT / reverse-charge treatment. Run-100 resolved `vq-092` for country screening: keep Croatia as incomplete/partial with conservative ordinary self-employment stress tests; exact `pausalni obrt`, contribution-band, VAT/reverse-charge, and family allowance fit is application-prep/accountant work.

#### Tax residence and scope

Croatian tax residents are taxed on worldwide income, while non-residents pay Croatian tax on Croatian-source income [src-306]. PwC's Croatia residence summary says an individual is a resident if they have real estate at their disposal in Croatia for at least 183 days in one or two calendar years, or are physically present in Croatia for at least 183 days in one or two calendar years; treaty tie-breakers may still matter where another country also claims residence [src-307]. For this couple, living in Croatia as a real base should therefore be treated as Croatian tax-residence planning, not just visa planning.

#### Ordinary self-employment / freelancer baseline

PwC treats self-employment income as annual income subject to progressive local-rate PIT and annual assessment [src-306][src-308]. Self-employment income includes small business / craft activity and independent professions, requires business books and evidence, and the tax base is receipts minus allowable expenses and personal allowances [src-308]. The annual lower PIT bracket covers the first EUR 60,000 of tax base; local governments set the lower rate within 15%-23% and the higher rate within 25%-33% depending on municipality type, with default rates of 20% and 30% if no local choice is made [src-306]. Zagreb can set up to 23% / 33%, while a municipality can be as low as 15% / 25% [src-306].

PwC lists a EUR 600/month personal allowance and EUR 300/month dependent-family-member allowance where eligible [src-309]. The tax example below uses only the worker's EUR 600/month allowance because partner-dependent treatment for an unmarried student and the exact filing posture are not verified. Croatia does not allow joint annual PIT filing [src-310].

#### Social contributions and health insurance

Croatia's social-security system covers pension and health insurance [src-310]. PwC gives the employee-style rates as 20% pension contributions and 16.5% health contributions; it also warns that contributions can be payable on realised income or an artificial base depending on the person's social-insurance status and circumstances [src-310]. For a solo foreign-client IT worker, the material gap is not the headline rates but the exact self-employed / craft contribution base and whether a lump-sum craft can use a fixed monthly contribution schedule. Until that is mapped by a Croatian accountant, use the ordinary calculation below as a conservative stress test, not the likely optimized outcome.

#### Lump-sum craft / small-business possibility

PwC confirms a derogation from book-based self-employment where a self-employed taxpayer is not subject to VAT and annual receipts do not exceed the VAT-registration threshold of EUR 60,000; in that case PIT can be paid as a lump sum based on a Tax Administration assessment [src-308][src-310]. This looks structurally important because USD 3,000/month is about EUR 31,152/year at the run-date FX rate [src-293], under EUR 60,000. However, this pass did not capture a clean official 2026 table for lump-sum income-tax bands, craft social contributions, IT activity eligibility, or foreign-client / cross-border service VAT treatment. Treat lump-sum craft as a promising but unscored optimization until verified.

#### VAT and filing mechanics

The general Croatian VAT rate is 25% [src-310]. The self-employment VAT-registration threshold is EUR 60,000 according to PwC's lump-sum-tax discussion [src-308]. Foreign-client IT services may involve reverse-charge or foreign-place-of-supply rules rather than charging Croatian VAT, but this was not verified in this pass; do not assume the exact invoicing treatment without a Croatian accountant. Annual PIT returns are due by the end of February for the previous year when the taxpayer realizes self-employment income; lump-sum self-employment uses a Tax Administration assessment and a special annual reporting form due within 15 days after year-end [src-310].

#### Worked example for USD 3,000/month

Run-date FX source `src-293` gives 1 USD = EUR 0.865342, so USD 3,000/month is about EUR 2,596/month or EUR 31,152/year. Conservative ordinary self-employment stress test:

| Item | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Gross receipts | EUR 31,152/year |
| Pension + health contributions stress test at 36.5% | EUR 11,371/year |
| Personal allowance | EUR 7,200/year |
| Tax base after contributions and allowance | EUR 12,582/year |
| PIT at 15% low-municipality lower rate | EUR 1,887/year |
| PIT at 20% default lower rate | EUR 2,516/year |
| PIT at 23% Zagreb maximum lower rate | EUR 2,894/year |
| Estimated net after contributions + PIT | about EUR 1,407-1,491/month |

This ordinary stress test is materially worse than Italy's forfetario-style result and may be overly conservative if Croatia's lump-sum craft route applies. The practical tax conclusion is therefore **incomplete rather than negative**: Croatia could be viable only if a Croatian accountant confirms a low-burden lump-sum craft / small-business setup, foreign-client VAT handling, and the exact 2026 contribution base. If the worker must use ordinary self-employment with broad contributions on receipts, the net at current income is tight.

#### Marriage / partner tax effects

Croatia has no joint annual PIT return [src-310]. A dependent-family-member allowance exists, but the pass did not verify whether an unmarried student partner qualifies, what proof is needed, or whether marriage materially changes allowance eligibility [src-309]. The first-pass example therefore does not include the partner allowance.

#### Practical tax playbook

1. Before choosing Croatia, ask a Croatian accountant to model two structures: ordinary book-based self-employment and lump-sum craft / small business.
2. Specifically verify whether foreign-client IT services can use lump-sum taxation while staying outside VAT at EUR 31,152/year, and how reverse-charge / place-of-supply invoicing works.
3. If the DN route becomes affordable later, re-check whether DN status changes Croatian tax treatment; this pass did not capture a tax exemption or special DN tax regime from a tax source.
4. Avoid basing the relocation budget on the optimistic lump-sum route until the 2026 contribution table and activity fit are documented.
5. For current-income planning, use the conservative ordinary-stress net of about EUR 1,407-1,491/month after Croatian tax/social contributions, before accountant fees and living costs.

## Cross-country observations

- Spain opens the 5.11 slice as a high-information but high-bureaucracy case: official route pages give useful timelines and filing channels, while the hard work is document localisation, appointment sequencing, tax/social-security setup, and relationship/income evidence.
- Portugal is a lower-SLA but still screenable case: AIMA gives a clear post-visa residence-card route for remote workers, while practical risk concentrates in the D8 income gate, appointment capacity, NIF / bank / lease setup, and tax-accounting execution.
- Italy adds a high-friction but screenable case: TP onboarding has a clear Questura first-contact baseline, while the DN route is paperwork-heavy and depends on a registered lease, post-arrival Questura permit, tax / `partita IVA` alignment, and Italian-speaking professional help.
- Greece adds the strongest explicit TP-to-ordinary-status practicality baseline in the slice so far: the ministry allows an electronic Immigration Code permit filing before 04 March 2027 without an entry visa for Greek TP holders, while the DN route remains consular and income-gated.
- Cyprus adds a clear but income-gated DN bureaucracy baseline: the filing office, 3-month arrival window, and 5-7 week examination time are captured, but lack of a Cyprus TP bridge and the EUR 3,500 net-income threshold keep it high-friction for this couple.
- Croatia adds a clear filing-channel baseline with online / consular / police-station DN options and concrete document-localisation requirements, but its 18-month DN cap, income floor, lack of captured TP bridge, and unresolved self-employment/craft setup keep it route-constrained.
- Malta closes the Tier-1-hint 5.11 pass with one of the clearest English online filing baselines, but also one of the weakest strategic reads: NRP is income-gated above the current budget, capped / non-settlement, and paired with rent / insurance pressure plus no captured TP bridge.
- Czech Republic opens the Tier-2 5.11 slice as a strong-but-execution-heavy case: official business-visa and special-residence mechanics are clearer than many peers, while translation/legalisation, self-employment evidence, flat-tax/accountant fit, health-insurance continuity, and current special-residence round timing remain practical gates.
- Poland adds the most Ukraine-specific Tier-2 practicality case so far: CUKR is concrete and local if eligibility holds, but the couple must choose the CUKR / marriage-family / business sequence before March 2027 and should not improvise ZUS, VAT, or voivode evidence without Polish professional support.
- Romania adds a lower-cost EU fallback with visible E-VISA / IGI workflows, but its practicality depends on solving the route mismatch: the DN threshold is above current income, D/AC is company/investment-heavy, and PFA/company plus family sequencing needs Romanian legal/accounting help before March 2027.
- Bulgaria adds a cost-attractive but route-constrained Tier-2 case: the official self-employment sequence is visible, but Employment Agency business-plan viability, the 3-year cap, no captured TP bridge, Bulgarian-language administration, and tax/NHIF/VAT fit make counsel/accountant coordination the decisive practicality issue.
- Hungary adds a nearby but fragile Tier-2 case: OIF / Enter Hungary mechanics are legible, but White Card income and family limits plus guest-self-employment tax/evidence complexity make a pre-2027 lawyer/accountant plan essential rather than optional.
- Slovakia adds a clear but adviser-dependent Tier-2 case: official business-residence mechanics and deadlines are visible, while the hard work is building a credible business-plan / real-business file, Slovak document localisation, SZCO/VAT/accountant fit, insurance onboarding, and marriage/family sequencing before TP expiry.
- Slovenia adds a safe but route-sequence-sensitive Tier-2 case: TP and DN filing channels are legible and services are strong, but the DN income gate, one-year non-renewable duration, self-employed tax/immigration fit, translations, insurance, lease evidence, and Slovenian-language administration require early professional coordination.
- Montenegro opens the non-EU Tier-2 5.11 pass as locally fileable but legally/tax-adviser-dependent: DN timing and MIA filing are visible, while income, partner, insurance, tax/SSC/VAT, lease, and PR-clock questions must be solved before treating it as a durable post-2027 base.
- Serbia adds a non-EU Tier-2 case where entry and electronic single-permit filing are relatively practical, but route classification is the hard part: the couple needs a credible self-employment / independent-professional tax and business file, landlord-supported address registration, and partner evidence before the visa-free or TP safety layer becomes a trap.
- Turkey adds a non-EU Tier-2 case that is easy to screen but income- and adviser-sensitive: PMM / e-ikamet and GoTurkey give visible filing anchors, yet USD 3,000/month is only the route floor, and the practical file depends on a residence-usable address, Turkish-language help, tax/SGK/VAT advice, insurance evidence, and marriage/family sequencing.
- Georgia adds an easy-entry but residence-file-sensitive Tier-2 case: SDA gives an IT residence / ordinary residence anchor that may fit the male partner, but the couple must build a small-business / tax / address / insurance / family-evidence file early rather than treating the one-year Ukrainian stay baseline as settlement.
- Albania adds a warm, lower-cost Balkan case where the bottleneck is not day-to-day affordability but official-route execution: WAF/capture gaps, Unique Permit classification, tax/VAT setup, insurance, lease/address proof, and marriage/dependent mechanics should be handled through Albanian professional support before treating it as durable.
- North Macedonia adds the next low-cost Balkan case where affordability is not enough: the couple needs a current self-employment/company residence plan, Macedonian-language document support, tax/VAT/contribution advice, and Skopje-first service sequencing before treating it as post-2027 durable.
- Bosnia and Herzegovina adds the clearest warning that low rent does not equal practical settlement: the visible ordinary route is Service / work-permit / company-file heavy, document-localised, entity-fragmented, and likely needs lawyer/accountant filtering before any post-2027 reliance.
- Moldova adds a nearby bridge/base case where bureaucracy is visible but route gates are unresolved: GIM and DN filing anchors are practical from Chisinau, yet the DN formula, partner sponsorship, PR-counting, tax/contribution category, and regional-security/Transnistria caveat must be managed before relying on it beyond TP.
- Future 5.11 slice passes should keep lawyer/contact entries as neutral leads rather than recommendations, and should separate route-screening sufficiency from final application-prep details such as quotes, appointment slots, and gestor engagement letters.
